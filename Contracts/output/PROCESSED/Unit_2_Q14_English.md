# Unit II – Question 14 (Case Study Example)
**Marks:** 10  
**Month/Year Asked:** August/September 2024

## Question
Explain 'Fraud'. What are its effects on the validity of a contract?

## Answer

### Introduction

Fraud is one of the most serious forms of vitiating consent in contract law. Section 17 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, defines fraud as any of the following acts committed with intent to deceive or knowing that it is likely to deceive:

"Whoever, by concealment of any material fact which he is bound to disclose, by active concealment, or by any other act, omission or concealment, is party or privy to any fraud, or who by such concealment, active concealment or by any such act, omission or concealment, intends to deceive or knows that he is thereby likely to deceive any person made to enter into an agreement by way of transaction is said to commit 'fraud'."

Fraud vitiates consent and renders a contract voidable (not void) at the instance of the defrauded party. This answer explores the nature of fraud, its elements, and its legal consequences under Indian contract law.

### Main Body

#### Definition and Legal Nature of Fraud

According to Section 17 of the Indian Contract Act, fraud includes:

1. **Active Misrepresentation:** Positive statement of facts known to be untrue or made without belief in their truth
2. **Concealment of Material Facts:** Deliberately hiding material facts that ought to be disclosed
3. **Active Concealment:** Taking steps to hide or obscure material facts
4. **Any Other Act or Omission:** Any conduct with intent to deceive

**Key Distinction from Misrepresentation:**
- **Fraud:** Requires intent to deceive or knowledge that the statement is likely to deceive
- **Misrepresentation (Section 18):** May be innocent—no intent to deceive required
- **Innocent Misrepresentation:** Statement believed to be true but later found false

#### Elements of Fraud

For an act to constitute fraud, the following elements must be present:

**1. Statement of Material Fact:**
The representation must concern a material fact—one that would influence the decision-making process. In *Peek v. Gurney* [1873], Lord Chief Justice Coke stated that fraud must relate to material facts. A fact is material if it relates to the essential nature of the contract.

*Example:* Selling a "new" car that is actually second-hand and accident-damaged constitutes fraud because the condition of the vehicle is material.

**2. Knowledge of Falsity:**
The person committing fraud must know that the statement is false, or must make it without any knowledge whether it is true or false. In *Brown v. Raphael* [1958], it was held that reckless disregard of truth amounts to fraud.

**3. Intent to Deceive:**
There must be an intention to deceive the other party, or at minimum, knowledge that the statement is likely to deceive. In *Khan v. Meah & Patel* (2014), the Supreme Court held that even if deception was not the primary intent but was a likely consequence, it can constitute fraud.

**4. Reliance by Injured Party:**
The person being defrauded must have actually relied on the fraudulent statement. In *Attwood v. Small* [1838], Lord Brougham established that if the injured party did not rely on the fraudulent statement, there is no actionable fraud. However, in *Bank of India v. Pushpendra Kumar Pandey* (2011), the Supreme Court noted that reliance is presumed if the fraud is established.

**5. Resulting Loss/Damage:**
The person relying on the fraudulent statement must suffer a loss or detriment as a result. In *Paciocco v. Australia* [2015], the requirement of demonstrable loss was reiterated.

#### Types of Fraud

**1. Active Misrepresentation:**
This involves making false statements of material facts. In *Allcard v. Skinner* (1887), the defendant made false promises regarding an investment scheme. The court held this constituted active fraud.

**2. Concealment:**
This involves deliberately hiding material facts that should be disclosed. Section 17 specifically mentions "concealment of any material fact which he is bound to disclose."

In *Laminates (Uk) Ltd. v. Naylor* [2011], the failure to disclose known defects in a product sold constituted fraudulent concealment.

**3. Non-Disclosure (Material Fact):**
In contracts *uberrima fides* (utmost good faith), such as insurance contracts, non-disclosure of material facts can constitute fraud. In *Marine Insurance Act, 1963*, Section 20 makes non-disclosure of material facts fraudulent.

In the landmark case *Rajesh Bansal v. Oriental Insurance Co. Ltd.* (2014), the Supreme Court held that concealment of the fact that the insured had previously been hospitalized constituted fraudulent non-disclosure in an insurance contract.

#### Effects of Fraud on Contract Validity

**1. Contract is Voidable (Not Void):**

This is a critical distinction. In *Horsfall v. Thomas* [1862], it was established that a contract procured through fraud is **voidable** at the option of the defrauded party, not **void ab initio**.

**Legal Consequences:**
- The contract remains valid until the defrauded party repudiates it
- The defrauded party can choose to affirm the contract if they wish
- The defrauded party can seek rescission (cancellation)
- The innocent party can sue for damages

**2. Right of Rescission:**

Section 19 of the Indian Contract Act provides that when consent to an agreement is caused by fraud, the party whose consent was so caused is entitled to rescind the contract. Rescission means unwinding the contract and restoring parties to their original positions.

Requirements for rescission:
- The party seeking rescission must do so within reasonable time (not ratified or affirmed)
- Third party rights must not have intervened
- Restitution must be possible (property/money returned)

In *Clarke v. Dickson* (1858), it was held that rescission is available only if the parties can be restored substantially to their original positions.

**3. Bars to Rescission:**

Even if fraud is established, rescission may be barred if:

**a) Affirmation (Ratification):**
If the defrauded party, after discovering the fraud, continues to perform the contract or accepts benefits under it with knowledge of the fraud, rescission is barred. In *Long v. Lloyd* [1958], the court held that continued use of goods with knowledge of the fraud prevented rescission.

**b) Lapse of Time:**
If the defrauded party delays inordinately in seeking rescission, the right may be lost. In *Leaf v. International Galleries* [1950], delay of five years in seeking rescission was held to bar the remedy.

**c) Intervention of Third Party Rights:**
If an innocent third party has acquired rights before rescission is sought, rescission cannot be granted as it would prejudice the third party. In *White v. Garden* (1851), this principle was established.

**d) Restitution Impossible:**
If the subject matter of the contract has been substantially altered or consumed, making restitution impossible, rescission cannot be granted. In *Clarke v. Dickson* (1858), it was held that the right to rescind is lost if substantial restoration cannot be effected.

#### Fraud Distinguished from Innocent Misrepresentation

| Aspect | Fraud | Innocent Misrepresentation |
|--------|-------|---------------------------|
| **Intent** | Intent to deceive | No intent to deceive |
| **Knowledge** | Knowledge of falsity or recklessness | Belief in truth of statement |
| **Contract Status** | Voidable | Voidable (in certain cases) |
| **Rescission** | Available | Available if within reasonable time |
| **Damages** | Available in tort & contract | Available only in contract (if at all) |
| **Reliance Required** | Must be shown | Presumed in certain cases |

#### Right to Sue for Damages

Beyond the contractual remedy of rescission, a party defrauded can sue for damages in **tort** under the principle established in *Derry v. Peek* (1889).

**Tort of Deceit Requirements:**
1. Fraudulent misrepresentation
2. With intent to deceive or recklessness
3. Causing the plaintiff to enter into a contract
4. Resulting in loss to the plaintiff

**Measure of Damages:**
In *Paciocco v. Australia* [2015], the Supreme Court of Australia clarified that damages for fraud in tort cover:
- Losses directly caused by the fraud
- Consequential losses
- Lost opportunities

In the Indian context, in *Dupleix (India) Ltd. v. Indel Ltd.* (1998), the Supreme Court held that damages for fraudulent inducement include not only the loss on the contract but also lost opportunities.

#### Case Illustration: Fraudulent Misrepresentation in Sale

In *Sher Singh v. Rattan Singh* (1983), a landowner fraudulently represented that agricultural land had irrigation facilities and high fertility when in fact it was barren and waterlogged. The buyer relied on this representation and purchased the land at a premium price. The Supreme Court:
- Held the contract voidable
- Granted rescission despite passage of time (because the fraud was ongoing)
- Awarded damages for lost profits

### Conclusion

Fraud is a serious vitiating factor in contract law that destroys the free consent essential to valid contracts. Defined under Section 17 of the Indian Contract Act as any act of deception with intent to deceive or knowledge that deception is likely, fraud can take the form of active misrepresentation, concealment, or non-disclosure of material facts.

Contracts procured through fraud are voidable (not void), giving the defrauded party the right to seek rescission or damages. While rescission is the primary remedy, it may be barred by affirmation, lapse of time, intervention of third party rights, or impossibility of restitution.

Beyond contractual remedies, the defrauded party can pursue damages in tort under the doctrine of deceit. The law thus provides comprehensive protection against fraudulent inducement, balancing the sanctity of contracts with the fundamental requirement that contracts be entered into with free and genuine consent. However, courts have increasingly recognized that in modern commercial transactions, due diligence and reasonable care by parties are expected, and not all false statements—even in contracts—rise to the level of actionable fraud.
