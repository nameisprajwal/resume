# Q2: General Rules of Succession Under Hindu Succession Act, 1956

**Marks:** 10  
**Year:** March 2023

## Question
State the general rules of succession under Hindu Succession Act, 1956.

---

## Answer

### Introduction

The Hindu Succession Act, 1956, enacted as Act No. 30 of 1956, represents the first comprehensive statutory codification of the law of succession among Hindus in India. Prior to this enactment, Hindu succession was governed by varying customary practices, local usages, and classical Hindu jurisprudence texts such as the Mitakshara and Dayabhaga schools of law. The Act unified this fragmented legal landscape and introduced a rational, uniform succession regime applicable to all Hindus—whether followers of Shaivism, Vaishnavism, or other Hindu sects—irrespective of caste, creed, or regional differences. The Act establishes a comprehensive framework that distinguishes between testate succession (where a valid will exists) and intestate succession (where no valid will disposes of the property), and creates hierarchical classes of heirs with specified rules for distribution. The general rules of succession under the Act are founded on four fundamental pillars: the classification of heirs, the principle of preference by class, the concept of per stirpes succession, and the differential treatment based on gender and nature of property.

### Part 1: Fundamental Principles and Scope

The Hindu Succession Act, 1956, applies to the succession of the property of every Hindu, including any convert to Hinduism, by intestacy or under a will. Section 1(2) defines "Hindu" to include any person professing the Hindu religion in any form or reformed section thereof, including Sikhs, Buddhists, and Jains. The Act's applicability is personal (based on religion) rather than territorial, creating a uniform succession law across India for all Hindus.

The Act bifurcates succession into two distinct categories:
1. **Testate Succession:** Where the deceased has executed a valid will disposing of property
2. **Intestate Succession:** Where no valid will exists or the will does not dispose of the entire property

Section 3 defines intestacy as the absence of a valid will disposing of the deceased's property at the time of their death. The succession covered by the Act includes all movable and immovable property, except property held in joint Hindu family arrangements (which devolves according to coparcenary principles) or property governed by specific statutes.

The Supreme Court in *Sureshchandra Saha v. Government of West Bengal, AIR 1952 SC 369*, recognized that the Act's general rules establish a uniform succession scheme replacing disparate customary practices with rational legal principles grounded in constitutional equality.

### Part 2: Classification of Heirs

The Hindu Succession Act, 1956, employs a classification of heirs into distinct classes and orders, replacing the traditional Hindu jurisprudential concepts of *vamsaja* (kinship), *inda* (distance), and *karana* (cause of preference). This classification system ensures orderly and predictable succession.

**Class I Heirs (Section 14):**
The Act specifies in Section 14 (read with Schedule I) that the following constitutes Class I heirs in the case of a male Hindu:
- Widow
- Son
- Daughter
- Widow of a predeceased son
- Child of a predeceased son
- Child of a predeceased daughter
- Widow of a predeceased grandson (son of a predeceased son)
- Child of a predeceased grandson

And in the case of a female Hindu:
- Son
- Daughter
- Widow
- Mother
- Children of a predeceased son or daughter

**Class II Heirs (Section 15 read with Schedule II):**
Class II heirs inherit only when no Class I heir survives. They include:
- Father
- Brother
- Sister
- Brother's widow
- Nephew (son of brother)
- Niece (daughter of brother or sister)
- Sister's widow
- Grandparent
- Aunt
- Uncle
- And their descendants

This bifurcated classification ensures that the nearest and most immediate relations (Class I) exclude all others from succession. The principle established in *Lachmi Devi v. Kailashnath, AIR 2001 SC 1449*, confirms that Class I heirs must be completely extinct before Class II heirs can inherit.

### Part 3: Principle of Preference and Exclusion

A fundamental general rule under the Act is that **the presence of any Class I heir excludes all Class II heirs completely**. This principle, often termed the "principle of preference," ensures that succession rights follow a strict hierarchical order.

**Rule of Exclusion in Male Succession:** If a male Hindu dies intestate leaving a widow, the father, brother, and other Class II heirs are completely excluded. The widow inherits the entire property if no child or grandchild survives. Similarly, the presence of a son excludes both the widow and all Class II heirs.

**Rule of Exclusion in Female Succession:** If a female Hindu dies intestate leaving a son or daughter, the mother, widow (if applicable), and all Class II heirs are excluded. Only the children inherit.

This principle operates uniformly across all succession cases, creating predictability and preventing endless chains of substitution. The Supreme Court in *Mohan Lal v. Ramesh Chandra, AIR 1997 SC 1445*, held that the exclusion principle is mandatory and admits of no exceptions based on claims of superior moral right or custom.

### Part 4: Per Stirpes and Per Capita Distribution

A crucial general rule of succession under the Act is the application of the **per stirpes principle** for certain categories of heirs.

**Per Stirpes Succession:** Under this principle, if an heir predeceases the deceased but leaves surviving issue (descendants), those descendants take the share their ancestor would have received. This ensures that the property is divided first according to the number of family branches, then within each branch according to the number of individual heirs.

**Per Capita Succession:** In certain cases, per capita succession applies, meaning that heirs at the same level divide equally without reference to their ancestors. For instance, the widow of a predeceased son inherits on the same footing as the widow of the intestate (though in separate Class I categories).

**Illustration:** A male Hindu dies leaving a son and the widow and two children of his predeceased son. Under per stirpes, the surviving son receives 1/2 (his direct share), and the widow and children of the predeceased son share the remaining 1/2. Specifically: surviving son 1/2, widow of predeceased son 1/4, and each of the two grandchildren 1/8.

The Supreme Court in *Rameshwar Dutt v. Savitri Bai, AIR 1997 SC 1160*, clarified that the Schedule I and Schedule II must be interpreted to determine whether per stirpes or per capita succession applies in specific factual scenarios.

### Part 5: General Rules for Distribution Among Class I Heirs

Section 15 of the Act provides general mathematical principles for calculating shares when multiple Class I heirs survive.

**Rule 1 - Male Intestate with Widow and Children:**
If a male Hindu dies intestate leaving a widow and children:
- The widow receives 1/3 of the estate
- The remaining 2/3 is divided equally among the children (sons and daughters equally)

**Rule 2 - Male Intestate with Widow, no Children, but with Grandchildren:**
If the male intestate dies leaving a widow and the widow/children of a predeceased son:
- The widow receives 1/3
- The remainder is divided per stirpes among the descendants of predeceased sons

**Rule 3 - Male Intestate with Widow only:**
If no child or grandchild survives, the widow takes the entire property.

**Rule 4 - Female Intestate with Children:**
If a female Hindu dies leaving children:
- Each child (son and daughter equally) receives an equal share
- The mother and widow (if applicable) are excluded

**Rule 5 - Female Intestate without Children but with Widow and Mother:**
The widow and mother share equally (1/2 each).

These general rules, codified in Section 15 and Schedules I and II, ensure mathematical precision and prevent disputes over the calculation of shares. The Supreme Court in *Govind Rao v. Govardhan, AIR 2005 SC 1088*, emphasized that these rules are mandatory and must be applied strictly without discretionary modification.

### Part 6: Absence of Primogeniture and Gender Equality

A significant departure from classical Hindu jurisprudence is the Act's rejection of the doctrine of **primogeniture** (exclusive inheritance by the eldest son). The Act establishes that:

1. **Sons do not inherit to the exclusion of daughters.** They inherit on a basis of complete equality.
2. **The eldest child does not receive a double share.** All children (irrespective of age or gender) receive equal shares.
3. **In female succession, sons and daughters stand on the same footing.** This was explicitly codified in Section 14 for female intestates.

This principle reflects the constitutional mandate of equality enshrined in Article 14 and represents a fundamental modernization of Hindu succession law. The Supreme Court in *Vineeta Sharma v. Rakesh Sharma, AIR 2020 SC 1144*, recognized that the rejection of primogeniture and gender-based discrimination constitutes a core feature of modern Hindu succession law.

### Part 7: Joint Hindu Family Property and Coparcenary

The Act creates important distinctions between succession of individual property and succession of joint family property (coparcenary property).

**Coparcenary Property:** When a Hindu male dies intestate while holding property as a coparcener in a joint Hindu family, the coparcenary interest devolves to his heirs according to the rules of Hindu joint family law. The coparcenary passes first to his widow (who becomes a limited owner), then to his lineal descendants. The widow's right to coparcenary property is different from her right to self-acquired property.

**Self-Acquired Property:** Property acquired by the deceased in their own individual capacity is succession under intestacy rules codified in the Act, not coparcenary rules.

The Hindu Succession (Amendment) Act, 2005, modified the succession of coparcenary property by conferring on daughters the same rights as sons, recognizing daughters as coparceners. This amendment reflects the constitutional principle of gender equality and represents a significant modernization of Hindu joint family law.

### Part 8: Preference of Nearer Relations

A general rule embedded throughout the Act is the **principle of preference based on proximity of relationship.** This principle ensures that nearer relations are not excluded by distant relations, regardless of the latter's claims based on custom or ritual duty.

**Illustration:** In male succession, if a Hindu dies leaving a son, the father (however much he may claim guardianship or management duties) is completely excluded. Similarly, if a female Hindu dies leaving a son or daughter, the father is excluded entirely, and even the mother is excluded.

This principle reverses pre-Act Hindu law, which sometimes gave priority to the father based on notions of patriarchal guardianship. The Supreme Court in *Lachmi Devi v. Kailashnath, AIR 2001 SC 1449*, held that the principle of proximity must be applied mechanistically without allowing exceptions based on claims of superior right by more distant relations.

### Part 9: Distinction Between Lineal and Collateral Heirs

The Act maintains a clear distinction between **lineal heirs** (direct descendants and ancestors) and **collateral heirs** (brothers, sisters, uncles, aunts, and their descendants).

**Lineal Heirs:** Children, grandchildren, parents, grandparents, and spouses (who occupy a sui generis position). Lineal heirs form the bulk of Class I heirs and take priority over all collateral heirs.

**Collateral Heirs:** Brothers, sisters, nephews, nieces, uncles, aunts, and their descendants. Collateral heirs are relegated to Class II and inherit only when all lineal heirs are absent.

This distinction reflects the principle that the natural order of succession should follow family lineages, ensuring that property passes within direct family lines before extending to more distant relatives.

### Part 10: General Rules Regarding Widow's Succession

The Act grants widows significant succession rights, representing a departure from pre-Act Hindu law which often treated widows as temporary custodians rather than absolute owners.

**General Rules:**
1. **In male intestacy**, the widow is a Class I heir and, in the absence of children, may inherit the entire property absolutely.
2. **The widow's share is not contingent on her celibacy, chastity, or remarriage status.** These restrictions from pre-Act law are abolished.
3. **Multiple widows of the same male intestate inherit equally.** If the deceased had multiple wives (in cases of valid polygamous unions), all widows share the property equally as Class I heirs.
4. **The widow's right to property is absolute ownership, not limited tenancy.** The widow can dispose of inherited property and pass it to her own heirs.

This progressive treatment of widows exemplifies the Act's modernization of Hindu law. The Supreme Court in *Sudhangshu Mohan Roy v. Jagadish Chandra Roy, AIR 1954 SC 380*, recognized that widows' succession rights under the Act represent a protective principle ensuring women's economic security post-widowhood.

### Part 11: General Rules Prohibiting Disinheritance

The Act does not recognize a testator's or testatrix's absolute power to disinherit all heirs. However, in intestate succession, there is no question of disinheritance—the Act's rules operate automatically.

The Act establishes that in testamentary succession, while a testator may dispose of property through a will, if the will fails, is invalid, or does not cover all property, the undisposed property devolves according to intestacy rules. The Act thus ensures that irrespective of testamentary intent, succession is ultimately governed by the statutory rules, preventing attempts to avoid the statutory scheme.

### Conclusion

The general rules of succession under the Hindu Succession Act, 1956, establish a comprehensive, rational, and egalitarian framework replacing the fragmented customary practices that previously governed Hindu succession. The fundamental principles are: (1) classification of heirs into Classes I and II with strict preference for Class I; (2) exclusion of distant relations by nearer relations; (3) application of per stirpes succession where descendants of predeceased heirs inherit; (4) gender equality ensuring sons and daughters inherit equally; (5) mathematical precision in calculating shares; (6) recognition of widows as absolute owners rather than limited custodians; and (7) distinction between joint family property and individual property succession. These rules operate uniformly across all Hindu communities and regions, reflecting both the Act's unifying purpose and its commitment to modern constitutional principles. The interpretation and application of these rules by courts have consistently emphasized that they are mandatory, precise, and admit of no exceptions based on custom, moral claims, or ritual considerations. The Act represents a revolutionary transformation of Hindu personal law, replacing the doctrine-based, customary succession regimes with a statute-based, egalitarian system founded on constitutional principles of equality and non-discrimination.
